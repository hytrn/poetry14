This poem always touches me deeply, whether it is the first time or in challenging moments. In fact, I loved this so much that I memorized and sung the version by Jean Coulthard, which is truly gorgeous.


Jorge Luis Borges, in his exploration of metaphor in literature, incorporated this very poem into his lecture. He highlights the challenge posed by its opening line, asserting that it resists systematic analysis. Borges contends that the extrapolations within the poem, while splendid, are ultimately unnecessary. The comparison, he suggests, is grasped implicitly, transcending the need for conscious thought. This notion aligns with Wallace Stevens' perspective, articulated in his poem "Man Carrying Thing," where he advocates for a poem that "resists the intelligence almost successfully." Both Borges and Stevens underscore the idea that certain elements of poetry operate beyond immediate intellectual understanding, relying on implicit comprehension and a nuanced engagement with the reader's emotions and intuition.6
