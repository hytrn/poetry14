Love

Moonlit Night

756

Du Fu

Illustration by Chelsea Feng / The China Project

In the midst of the profound darkness of political and societal upheaval, the poets of the Tang dynasty emerged as stars, casting their brilliance upon the night sky. Alongside his esteemed contemporary and friend Li Bai, Du Fu is frequently regarded as one of the prominent poets in Chinese literary history. While Li Bai is revered as the Poetry Immortal, Wang Wei as the Poetry Buddha, and Li He as the Poetry Devil, Du Fu is dubbed by Chinese scholars with the title of Poetry Sage. The breadth of his poetic repertoire has led Western readers to liken him to a multitude of literary giants, including Virgil, Horace, Ovid, Shakespeare, Milton, Burns, Wordsworth, Bรฉranger, Hugo, or Baudelaire.1


Born to a minor scholar-official in Henan Province, Du Fu faced early challenges with the loss of his mother and was raised with the assistance of an aunt. Despite preparing for the civil service exam to follow in his father's footsteps, Du Fu's attempts were unsuccessful, leading to years of extensive travel.2His early poetic works wove together narratives from his journeys, recounting personal hardships and expressing sentiments towards Li Po.


Du Fu sought an official position through government petitioning and was appointed as a registrar in the crown prince's palace. However, the An Lushan Rebellion in 755 thwarted his assumption of the role, marking a tumultuous eight-year period of societal upheaval in China. During this time, Du Fu lived an itinerant life, documenting through poetry the events he witnessed, including famine, political turbulence, and personal tragedies.3


Despite eventually securing the position of registrar, Du Fu's conscientious approach led to demotion. He later relocated to Sichuan, where he dwelled in a cottage and composed numerous poems depicting his comparatively contented life. His later years were marked by continued mobility, including a notable two-year period in Kuizhou, where he garnered the support of the regional governor and produced many poems in his characteristic dense style. Du Fu and his family resumed their travels in 768, and he passed away in Hunan Province, survived by his wife and two sons.


Du Fu is frequently characterized as a poet-historian, with his works reflecting the emotional resonance and significance of political and social issues, encompassing a spectrum of personal concerns, trials, and dramas. His poetry stands out for its diverse range of moods and thematic content. According to translator David Hinton, Du Fu "explored the full range of experience, and from this abundance shaped the monumental proportions of being merely human."4


In this dedicated poem to his wife, Du Fu employs tender and evocative language to intensify the yearning between husband and wife. The poem's central theme, focused on the moon, inherently evokes a sense of solitude and secrecy. Readers can deduce that Du Fu is situated in Changโan, separated from his family residing in Fuzhou. The verses hint at the deep memories created by the couple in Changโan, particularly during the ten-year confinement imposed by the An Lushan Rebellion, where they shared numerous hardships. Du Fu's juxtaposition of his children's innocence with the harsh realities of adulthood underscores the poignant moments of his past.


The poet further details the moon's interaction with his wife, expressing his longing to be reunited with her.
