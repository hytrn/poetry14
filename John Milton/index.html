Regret

On His Blindness

1655

John Milton

Photo by Stock Montage / Getty Images

Milton, born three years prior to the 1611 appearance of the King James Bible, received education at St. Paul’s and Cambridge. A staunch parliamentarian, he assumed the role of Secretary of Foreign Tongues for Cromwell’s Council of State in 1649, a post held until Charles II's Restoration in 1660.


Secluding himself thereafter, Milton confronted the encroaching blindness in 1650, beginning the epic creation of Paradise Lost,1and was fully blind two years later. Completing his magnum opus in 1663, he sold its copyright for £10.2 Yet, it elevated Milton’s reputation as one of the greatest poets in history.3Paradise Regained and Samson Agonistes followed in 1671, a time of extreme religious influx and political turmoil. A humorous, witty anecdote from one of my old English teachers, Mr. Allin, who suggested that, during the composition of these works, Milton, blind on his bed, dictated verses to each of his three daughters, blessing their marriages only upon the novels' completion.


Milton dismissed rhyme as ‘but the intervention of a barbarous age, to set off wretched matter and lame metre’4and talked of ‘the troublesome and modern bondage of rhyming,’ a sentiment he seems to have forgotten when writing the following sonnet. Through only sonnets can we as readers really understand Milton’s Personal struggles. This sonnet commemorates key events in his filled with extraordinary creatively ambitions and self-discipline. Here, we can see attitudes so classic of Milton: a proud individualist fervently subordinates under God.


I chose not to include excerpts from the much better-known Paradise Lost, but this sonnet for a deep, personal significance. One of my first assignments for school in Canada for me was to recite a poem of my choice for ‘Language Art’ class. Wanting to make a good first impression, I did not want to present a Shakespearean sonnet, something that is all too common, and thus I picked Milton's, unbeknownst to me how biographer William Hayley regarded him as the “greatest English author.”5While classmates effortlessly navigated the assignment, with one even composing his own limerick,6 I stumbled through hideous phrases like ‘ere’ and ‘doth,’ grappling with the thick Vietnamese accent at the time. Looking back as a senior though, I dare say “my light is [well] spent.”

When I consider how my light is spent,
Ere half my days, in this dark world and wide,
And that one Talent which is death to hide
Lodged with me useless, though my Soul more bent
To serve therewith my Maker, and present
My true account, lest he returning chide;
“Doth God exact day-labour, light denied?”
I fondly ask. But patience, to prevent
That murmur, soon replies, “God doth not need
Either man’s work or his own gifts; who best
Bear his mild yoke, they serve him best. His state
Is Kingly. Thousands at his bidding speed
And post o’er Land and Ocean without rest:
They also serve who only stand and wait.

It has also left its mark on Mary Shelley’s Frankenstein and influenced the works of Percy Bysshe Shelley and William Blake; the latter having illustrated an edition of Paradise Lost.
