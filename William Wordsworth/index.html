Existence

Daffodil

1807

William Wordsworth

Hulton Archive / Getty Images

William Wordsworth, considered the flagship of Romanticism, was born in the picturesque town of Cockermouth, nestled in the enchanting English Lake District. His poetic portrayal of the Dove River in his hometown contributed to its popularity as a tourist destination, even during his lifetime. After studying at Cambridge and a brief sojourn in France, the tumultuous consequences of the French Revolution compelled him to return to England.1Reflecting on these experiences, he sought solace and produced notable works during this period, including an early version of his autobiographical masterpiece, The Prelude (1905, extensively revised in 1915).2In 1798, he, along with Samuel Taylor Coleridge3, published Lyrical Ballads, a collection often regarded as the cornerstone of English Romanticism. Revered by the public, Wordsworth ascended to the position of Poet Laureate4in 1843.


Lyric poems, historically associated with brevity, musicality, and the expression of intense emotions, trace their origins back to ancient Greece. Initially, the term lyric denoted a short poem sung by a solo performer with a lyre.56Notably, specific types of lyrics, such as the ode7and the elegy,8originated in the ancient world. Although the concept of lyric poetry has evolved, retaining associations with short forms and subjective expression, the boundaries have become less distinct. While not all modern lyrics are intended to be sung, let alone to a lyre, they continue to focus on the inward experiences, emotions, and thoughts of a single first-person speaker. When engaging with lyric poetry, the emphasis often lies in comprehending the speaker's situation and the vivid relevance of their internal state to readers—a deliberate mastery demonstrated by Wordsworth, unlike Keats.9


The pleasure of nature, for Wordsworth, is inherently retrospective, a recurring theme in his poetry. As always, Wordsworth emerges as a poet of consciousness as much as of nature. Also notice in this poem how, in the context of the Industrial Revolution’s onset, Wordsworth used the term “wealth” to characterize the benefits derived from the sight of nature. He suggests that, amidst this transformative era, a greater wealth can be found in the natural world and in the interpretations the mind makes from it.


It's intriguing to note that Wordsworth's encounter with the daffodils wasn't a solitary experience. Accompanied by his sister Dorothy, he drew from her journal entries, incorporating her language into the fabric of his poem. Additionally, the collaborative nature of the poem extends further, with Wordsworth's wife contributing the memorable line "which is the bliss of solitude." In essence, this poem becomes a harmonious composition, woven together through the collective insights of Wordsworth, his sister, and his wife.10


One amusing memory that still sticks with me dates back to my seventh-grade days. In an English class, Mr. Allin posed a clarifying question: “What’s a daffodil?” To the collective amusement of the entire class, one earnest student confidently replied, “A bird!” From that day on, Mr. Allin made it a routine to ask this question, ensuring that everyone was well aware that daffodils are flowers before delving into any analysis.

I wandered lonely as a cloud
That floats on high o'er vales and hills,
When all at once I saw a crowd,
A host, of golden daffodils;
Beside the lake, beneath the trees,
Fluttering and dancing in the breeze.

Continuous as the stars that shine
And twinkle on the milky way,
They stretched in never-ending line
Along the margin of a bay:
Ten thousand saw I at a glance,
Tossing their heads in sprightly dance.

The waves beside them danced; but they
Out-did the sparkling waves in glee:
A poet could not but be gay,
In such a jocund company:
I gazed—and gazed—but little thought
What wealth the show to me had brought:

For oft, when on my couch I lie
In vacant or in pensive mood,
They flash upon that inward eye
Which is the bliss of solitude;
And then my heart with pleasure fills,
And dances with the daffodils.
