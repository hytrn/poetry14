Regret

Autumn Evening

Late Tang

Du Mu

(Public Domain)

Du Mu, born in Tang capital Chang'an to the declining Jingzhao Du clan during the late Tang period, passed the jinshi1degree in 828, starting a bureaucratic career. As the grandson of the esteemed historian Du You, he had a deep knowledge of Confucian and military classics. He held minor positions and faced obstacles in reaching higher office, partly due to family setbacks and political intrigues of the time.2His frustration is evident in his poetry, and he regularly wrote letters critiquing government policies and military strategies to those in power. Just before his death, he secured the role of Drafter in the Secretariat. Like many scholar-officials, Du Mu excelled not only in poetry but also in calligraphy, painting, and essays.


He had a penchant for discussing military matters, evident in works such as Zui Yan (literally, ‘Talk on Crimes’) and his commentary on Sun Tzu's The Art of War. Infused with military tactics, Du Mu's poems displayed audacity and vigor, characterized by bold and unrestrained expression. Du Mu was called "Little Du" to distinguish him from Du Fu , "Big Du". His poetic acclaim rivaled that of Li Shangyin,3leading later generations to refer to the two poets collectively as “Little Li Du”–the duo became the Li Bai and Du Fu of their time.4


As a child, I was exposed to numerous references to the Qixi Festival, which is the Chinese version of Valentine’s Day, on TV, and it's kind of strange how many kids' shows focus so much on the theme of boy-girl love! In recent years, the popularity of this event has only increased. I'm well-versed in the myth associated with the festival, which celebrates the annual meeting of the star-crossed lovers, the Cowherd (Niulang) and the Weaver Girl (Zhinü). The festival is observed on the seventh day of the seventh lunar month on the Chinese lunisolar calendar.


Despite their deep affection, their love was forbidden, leading to their banishment to opposite sides of the heavenly river, symbolizing the Milky Way. Once a year, on the seventh day of the seventh lunar month, a flock of magpies, responsible for their separation, would form a bridge to reunite the lovers for a single day. This narrative kindled my passion for astronomy, where the Cowherd and the Weaver Girl represent the Altair and Vega stars. This tale has been alluded to in various literary works, including a famous but not explicitly related poem. The poem tells of a lonely concubine at the palace whose fan loses its purpose as summer ends, believed to be an allusion by the poet expressing frustrations about his family's declining influence.


By utilizing a cold and distant choice of words along with vivid imagery, Du Mu evokes a sense of solitude in his poem. The implied focus of the composition is a youthful woman residing in opulence, hinted at by the mention of a silk fan—a luxury and a symbol of affluence in ancient China. Despite her elevated status, the poem suggests that rather than socializing with companions, she engages with fireflies and contemplates the stars alone during the nocturnal hours.5This portrayal unveils her as a solitary figure, living in seclusion for reasons undisclosed.


Du Mu reinforces this theme of isolation through the chilly atmosphere depicted in the poem, where terms like ‘冷’ (chill) and ‘凉’ (cool) connote a lack of vibrancy and vitality that accompanies the absence of others. The cause of her sequestered existence might be rooted in a broken heart, hinted at by the concluding lines that allude to the poignant Chinese legend of the Cowherd and the Weaving Maid—lovers separated by their disparate celestial and earthly statuses. Speculatively, if this maiden serves as a concubine to the emperor, she could be estranged from her imperial peers due to a childhood love. Although the exact cause of her isolation remains elusive, the poem unmistakably conveys the subject's profound sense of desolation.

Silvery candle, autumnal light, chills the painted screen
With small fan, of light silk gauze, she swipes at the flitting fireflies
On the palace steps, the night is cool, like water
Laying down, she gazes, the Cowherd and the Weaver Girl stars

Translated by Red Pine6

銀燭秋光冷畫屏，
輕羅小扇撲流螢。
天階夜色涼如水，
臥看牛郞織女星。

  1. 1The Jinshi exam was a prestigious imperial examination system in ancient China, established during the Sui dynasty and continued through the Tang dynasty. It served as the highest level of the civil service examination, testing candidates on their knowledge of classical Chinese literature and Confucian principles. Successfully passing the Jinshi exam granted individuals entry into the elite scholar-official class, offering opportunities for high-ranking positions in the imperial bureaucracy. The test typically occurred in the imperial capital within the palace and was alternatively known as the Metropolitan Exam.
  2. 2Nienhauser, William H. The Indiana Companion to Traditional Chinese Literature. Indiana University Press, 1986, pp. 824–826.
  3. 3Li Shangyin was a Chinese poet of the late Tang Dynasty, born around 813 and passing away in 858. Known for his distinctive and complex style, Li Shangyin's poetry often explores themes of love, longing, and the transient nature of life. Despite facing political turmoil during his lifetime, his works have endured and continue to be celebrated for their rich imagery and emotional depth in Chinese literary history.
