Nature

[To see a World in a Grain of Sand]

1803

William Blake

Painting by Thomas Phillips / National Potrait Gallery, London

William Blake, a Londoner, a poet, an engraver, an artist, and a visionary, published some of his best works almost a decade before Romanticism came into full swing with William Wordsworth and Samuel Taylor Coleridge’s heralded Lyrical Ballads. After studying at Henry Pars drawing school, he apprenticed as an engraver and attended the Royal Academy. Despite facing various trials, including one for treasonous speech, he remained married in 1782, acquitted of the charges.1After publishing his first book of poems, Poetical Sketches, he began experimenting with what he called illuminated printing, where the images and text of each page were engraved in relief on copper, which was then used to print sheets that were then partly colored by hand, resulting in each book carrying a unique beauty with no two precisely alike. Toward the end of his life, he devoted to pictorial art, illustrating The Canterbury Tales2, the Book of Job3, and The Divine Comedy.45


In contrast to William Blake's other renowned poems, this particular epigraph of the Auguries of Innocence, remained hidden in notebooks for many years after his death. It wasn't until 1863 that it saw its first publication. So, despite the widespread familiarity of these four lines, closely tied to Blake's legacy, they only emerged into public view long after his other significant works like London, The Tyger, and Milton A Poem (which includes the verses later adapted into the hymn Jerusalem) had already been published.6


Why did I opt for this unassuming epigraphic quatrain from a less widely recognized work, eschewing his more renowned volumes? In seventh-grade, my first year in high school (high-school on PEI starts at seventh grade) and my second year in Canada, I was given this poem as part of my English mid-term exam to analyze. Gratefully, the language used in this piece proved accessible, although I stilled grappled with guessing the meaning of the word ‘palm,’ erroneously associating it, as a foreign student from a tropical locale, with a palm tree.


Yet, what I was able to articulate at that moment continues to resonate with me. In my written response, I offered, “In this cool quote, Blake says we should look at big things in the little things. We can't really get how super huge stuff like 'the world,' 'heaven,' 'infinity,' or 'eternity' works because our brains are very small and limited. But Blake thinks we can kind of get it if we closely look at really tiny stuff. Like, if we look at a grain of sand, we can might understand how the world works. And if we check out a beautiful flower, we can see the beauty of Heaven. Blake tells us to think about the really big things by looking at the super small things. It is about finding deep connections between the big and little things!” To this day, this poem remains one of my favorites, and hopefully it resonates with many of you as well.

To see a World in a Grain of Sand
And a Heaven in a Wild Flower
Hold Infinity in the palm of your hand
And Eternity in an hour
