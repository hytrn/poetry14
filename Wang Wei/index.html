Nature

Deer Fence

High Tang

Wang Wei

Painting by Shangguan Zhou from 晩笑堂竹荘畫傳, published 1921.

Wang Wei, a distinguished figure in the middle Tang dynasty, was renowned for his multifaceted talents as a poet, painter, musician, and statesman. Embracing the humanist principles of a Chinese scholar-official, he served in various bureaucratic roles within the Tang court, navigating the challenges of the Anshi Rebellion (755–759) while mourning the loss of his wife and sister. Following these tribulations, he chose retirement in his countryside residence along the Wang River, where he delved deeper into the study of Buddhism, producing many profound poems.1


As the pioneer behind the Southern School of painter-poets, Wei left an enduring impact on the art world. His landscape paintings, characterized by monochrome and "pomo" techniques, explored the spiritual essence of nature.2Although none of his paintings have survived, the influence of his landscapes persisted through subsequent artists. In his poetry, Wei embodied Zen Buddhist ideals of detachment and simplicity, often depicting natural phenomena like water and mist. Anthologized alongside other Tang dynasty poets such as Li Po and Tu Fu, he is widely acknowledged for his sparing use of details.3


Wang Wei's prominence extended to his musical prowess, though not preserved, complementing his successful career as a court official. Despite authenticated examples of his paintings being absent, around 400 poems meticulously compiled by his younger brother, Wang Jin, under imperial order, showcase his deep connection with nature. His spiritual journey included a decade of study under Chán4master Daoguang, further enriching his multifaceted life. Wang Wei's legacy endures through his surviving poems, with 29 featured in the 18th-century anthology Three Hundred Tang Poems,5cementing his place as a prominent figure in arts and letters during the Tang dynasty.6


The concept conveyed here goes beyond a mere “deserted hills,” one lacking human presence or tangible objects, as indicated by the reference to a dense forest on the mountain. While this interpretation aligns with Wáng Wéi's perspective, it is plausible, given his Buddhist worldview, that the term '空' (empty) signifies a deeper emptiness in the Buddhist sense—a notion of the deceptive or illusory nature of existence, suggesting that all phenomena lack inherent reality. Considering Lù Zhái, a town name, served as one of Wáng's retreats, solitude may indeed have been his sought-after experience, validating the first interpretation. The inherent beauty of this poetic style lies in its ambiguity, allowing room for diverse interpretations and personal reflections, given that the poet's exact intentions remain elusive.


The lush green hues of the sky and clouds embody a sense of seamless harmony. Each nuanced color invokes contemplation of a taciturn domain, reminiscent of the transcendental realm found in meditation. The mountains stand devoid of human presence, their solitude echoing with distant voices. Sunlight filters through the dense forest, casting a gentle glow on the vibrant green moss. The colors here blend with light, its intensity softened by layers of space, creating a heightened sense of surreal beauty. Moreover, the cool, profound quality of the moss's green, paired with the subtle light, crafts a serene, celestial aesthetic, enriching the stillness and purity of the painting—an authentic portrayal of a secluded haven, a haven for meditation.

No one is seen in deserted hills,
only the echoes of speech are heard.
Sunlight cast back comes deep in the woods
and shines once again upon the green moss.

Translated by Stephen Owen7

空山不見人，
但聞人語響。
返景入深林，
復照青苔上。

Influenced by Daoist philosophy, Chán Buddhism, known as Zen in Japan, has left a lasting impact with its emphasis on sudden enlightenment and integration of Buddhist principles with a naturalistic worldview.
