“Immortal by the River”—Returning at Night to Linggao

1082

Su Shi

Photo by Keren Su. A stone sculpture of Su Shi in Meishan, Sichuan Province, China.

Art critic Alastair Sooke describes Su Shi(h)'s remarkable and diverse career during the Song dynasty, a period known for its artistic refinement in China.1Su Shi, a pre-eminent scholar of the era, excelled as a poet, politician, writer, calligrapher, painter, and aesthetic theorist. Sooke suggests the term "proto-Renaissance man" to capture Su Shi's prolific contributions across various fields, despite being born centuries before Da Vinci.


To grasp Su's significance in Chinese culture, understanding the Song dynasty's context is crucial. This period (960-1279) marked a turning point with population growth, economic empowerment, and increased education. Sophia Zhou, a specialist in Chinese Paintings, emphasizes that the Song dynasty reached the pinnacle of artistic achievement in Chinese history.2


The Song Dynasty's governance, led by scholar officials chosen through a meritocratic examination system, fostered a flourishing of painting and calligraphy—essential arts for scholar-officials. Unlike before, these officials were now expected not only to appreciate art aesthetically but also to possess the skill to create it.3


Su Shi, also known as Su Dongpo, lived from January 8, 1037, to August 24, 1101. Engaging in various art types and fields from poetry, essays, politics, calligraphy, painting, gastronomy, and travel writing, he played a crucial role in the political landscape of his time. Su aligned himself with figures like Sima Guang and opposed the New Policies faction led by Wang Anshi.4


Having a lengthy bureaucratic career with various provincial roles and serving as a senior official at the central imperial court, Su experienced political exiles that fueled his creative output, particularly in poetry. Widely recognized as a master of classical Chinese literature, he written famous poems, lyrics, prose, and essays. His clear and insightful essay writing contributed significantly to the understanding of topics like 11th-century Chinese travel literature and the iron industry. Su Shi resided on a property known as Dongpo, the source of his literary pseudonym, which translates to 'Eastern Slope.’


Su's influence extended beyond his lifetime, with enduring popularity in China, Japan, and other nearby regions. Translations by Arthur Waley and Stephen Owen introduced his works to English-speaking audiences. In the realm of arts, Su was hailed as "the preeminent personality of the eleventh century."5


Even today, Su Shi remains a beloved and influential figure, appreciated by both intellectuals and the general public. Contemporary researchers acknowledge his profound impact on the spirits and thoughts of the Chinese people, comparing his cultural and philosophical influence to that of Mencius6and Zhuangzi.7


The first time Su Shih crossed my radar was ironically linked to food, quite a departure from his usual realms of poetry and politics. (Not because this name reminds me of the Japanese dish though!) I vividly recall a Thursday afternoon when my mom, freshly acquainted with a recipe from the local community center, prepared Dongpo pork for our family. It's amusing how Su earns the nickname 'Da Vinci of the East,' given his role as one of the four ancient gastronomes of China. His diverse writings touched on various topics, including cuisine, evident in the famous dish named after him in Hangzhou cuisine. Legend has it that Su Shi once got so engrossed in a game of Chinese chess8with an old friend that he forgot about the stewed pork he was cooking. The dish ended up thick-cooked and incredibly fragrant, serving as a testament to his culinary adventures. However, conflicting legends suggest that villagers might have named the pork dish in his honor after his passing, though no concrete evidence supports either claim.


In this spirit, I have included the following poem, penned in 1082 CE, this composition captures the time when Su Shi underwent demotion and resettlement to what is presently identified as the Huangzhou district in Hubei Province.9Despite facing political challenges, Su Shi retained a deep connection to the natural environment. The verses express his desire to relinquish the pursuit of status, fame, and wealth, opting instead for a life of solitude.10

Drinking at night on the Eastern Slope, I sobered up and got drunk again.
When I arrived back home, it was about midnight.
The young servant was snoring like thunder,
not responding, no matter how hard I knocked.
Supported by a walking stick, I listened to the river.
I have long regretted that this body does not belong to me.
When can I stop pursuing fame and money?
The night comes to an end, the wind stops, and the waves in the ravine die down.
Let me drift away on a small boat from now on,
and entrust the rest of my life to rivers and seas.

Translated by Qian Jia11

夜飲東坡醒復醉，
歸來彷彿三更。
家童鼻息已雷鳴。
敲門都不應，
倚杖聽江聲。
長恨此身非我有，
何時忘卻營營。
夜闌風靜縠紋平。
小舟從此逝，
江海寄餘生。
